In this project, we set out to find a simple, grounded RL post-training method for diffusion image generators. We made a few observations about the structure of diffusion flows that lead to Finite Difference Flow Optimization, a new algorithm that converges faster, reaches higher rewards, and produces fewer artifacts than current solutions.

Diffusion models fit the distribution of their training data conservatively. This means they assign non-zero likelihood to images that are near the data manifold but not on it, resulting in a distribution that envelops the natural image manifold but produces many non-image samples[Autoguidance]. This sort of smoothing isn't entirely bad, since it allows the model to generate unseen interpolations of training images, but it also produces the washed-out, artifacted outputs you get from a base model.

This motivates the use of methods to sharpen the distribution. The dominant approach is classifier-free guidance (CFG) that concentrates mass toward higher-density modes at inference time. This gives you a knob to trade diversity for sample quality. But it's a crude band-aid: you can adjust its strength but not the style or quality it imparts on the images, typically it results in an over-saturated look. We explore RL-based post-training as an alternative and find that it produces the same useful tradeoff while steering the model toward reward-specified behavior.

## Why Apply RL to Image Generation?

In LLMs, RL post-training is a standard tool today. Pretraining produces a model with broad coverage but a loose, unfocused output distribution. Post-training techniques like RLHF and RLVR then tighten that distribution onto useful outputs, which has driven major wins in alignment and reasoning. In image generation, CFG analogously concentrates mass from a pretrained distribution into desirable regions. Because of CFG's dominance, RL adoption in image generation has been slower than in language models. However, RL is an appealing alternative since it can be steered with arbitrary rewards from learned models or human preferences.

It isn't obvious how to apply RL to diffusion and flow models. Standard RL machinery was built for domains where model likelihoods are easily accessible, but these are notoriously difficult to estimate in continuous flows. Most existing approaches work around this by forcing the flow into a Markov decision process (MDP) framework with ungrounded proxy likelihoods. A natural question is whether we can ascend the reward gradient, the stated goal of policy gradient methods, in a more flow-native way.

Previously, I (David) worked on applying policy gradients to flow matching for continuous control with FPO[cite], and for this project I joined the NVIDIA Helsinki team, which has recently focused on analyzing and improving the pretraining recipe for diffusion image generators (EDM, EDM2). FDFO is a simple first step toward an improved recipe for RL post-training of image generators.

## Background: Image Generator Post-Training

To introduce our method, let's start with a motivating example. A flow matching model generates images by starting from random noise and taking a sequence of denoising steps to arrive at a clean image. Suppose we have a reward model, say, a vision-language model (VLM) that we ask 'Does this image match the caption [CAPTION]? Answer Yes or No.' We can then use the likelihood of the 'Yes' token as a scalar reward. Our goal is to update the flow model so that it produces images that score higher under this reward (i.e., match the caption). By optimizing this reward, we're pushing the model's output distribution to concentrate on real images, which is exactly the kind of distribution sharpening we’re hoping to get from post-training. This is natural to formulate as an RL problem.

## Background: The Denoising MDP
Currently, the most popular algorithm for RL post-training image generators is the denoising Markov decision process (MDP). Introduced in DDPO and adopted by Flow-GRPO and DanceGRPO, it casts the multi-step sampling process as an MDP, where each denoising step is a separate action. While sampling images (rollouts), stochastic noise is injected at each step. If the corresponding image scores well, the random perturbations along that trajectory are reinforced. This means pulling the flow toward the perturbations if the image received high reward and away if it received low reward.

Standard policy gradient methods like PPO[cite] require likelihoods for each action. In a flow model, the velocity at each step is continuous, and computing its exact likelihood under the flow is infeasibly expensive. The denoising MDP recasts each step's perturbation as a sample from a Gaussian, which provides a proxy likelihood for PPO. This is simple, but it doesn’t reflect how likelihoods actually evolve under the flow (e.g., how the flow expands and contracts volume locally).

Credit assignment is also challenging under this formulation. When a trajectory leads to high reward, each perturbation along it gets reinforced, even though most were irrelevant to increasing reward. Much of the update is noise that pushes the flow in reward-neutral directions.

We decided to build an algorithm outside the denoising MDP framework and started by just looking for the most direct way to push a flow model's outputs toward higher reward.


## What if We Just Backprop It?

Let's return to our motivating example. For now, assume the reward is differentiable (we'll relax this later). Then, we can backpropagate through the VLM to get a gradient in image space. This represents the direction to shift the output image to produce greater reward. Flow models generate images through multiple denoising steps, each with its own predicted velocity, so we need to translate our image-space gradient into update directions for every velocity prediction along the sampling chain. The obvious way to do this is to backpropagate through the chain itself. Existing methods[cite ReFL, DRaFT, DRS] have explored and refined variants of this, but problems remain. The gradient has to traverse up each denoising step via the chain rule. Each step's Jacobian stretches, squeezes, and rotates the gradient in poorly controlled ways. By the time it reaches early steps, high frequencies are suppressed exponentially and some axes have exploded while others have vanished. Also, this assumes access to a differentiable reward model, which is a subset of the general RL case. In practice, this rules out rewards extracted from the real world, including from people.

It's worth analyzing what the backpropagation is doing mechanically. Each step's Jacobian transforms the gradient one step backward through the chain. Composing all of them brings the image-space gradient all the way up to the initial noise.

The plot below shows this for a 2D flow, where we can visualize how different gradient directions transform along the chain. We sample an initial noise $x_0$ and transport it to the data distribution (in this case, a fractal distribution) by integrating the flow step-by-step. A reward gradient (computed through a differentiable reward) is defined at the data sample and then carried up the sampling chain by multiplying by each step's Jacobian.

This chain of Jacobians gives us update directions for the velocity at every step, which we can train into the model, but it suffers from the conditioning problems (gradient squeezing/squashing) we described earlier. We propose a simple approximation that avoids these problems, based on the following observations about the geometry of the optimization and underlying flow:

1. <b>An update doesn't have to exactly match the gradient to be useful.</b> More precisely, a valid ascent direction is any direction at an angle of less than 90° from the true gradient, i.e., it lies in the same half-space. These ascent directions will still locally improve the reward, albeit less the steepest direction. In fact, it's a standard practice to update along directions that deviate from the true gradient even when it's available. Adam rescales the gradient per-coordinate, while Muon rebalances its principal components. Both lead to better optimization properties than naive SGD.

2. <b>Flow matching models are tuned to produce flows with minimal curvature,</b> since those require fewer steps at inference time. This straightness property is useful for us. Intuitively, if the flow is straight, then a gradient computed on the final sample will be a valid ascent direction for intermediate steps along the trajectory. Mathematically, this corresponds to the Jacobian of the flow being positive semi-definite: it can stretch and scale, but it doesn't rotate a gradient more than 90°. Optimal transport flows, the theoretical ideal, have exactly positive semi-definite Jacobians. While learned flows aren't equivalent to optimal transport, it is still a reasonable approximate mental model.


## A Simple Alternative

This motivates a simple alternative: instead of backpropagating through the sampling chain, just copy the image-space gradient up to all earlier steps. The argument from the previous section suggests this is a valid direction at each step, as long as the flow isn't too rotational.

## Extending to Non-Differentiable Rewards

Everything so far still requires a differentiable reward model to compute the image-space gradient. We want to make our method compatible with any scalar reward, including human feedback, external tools and black-box metrics, which is the general RL scenario.

So far, we've assumed that the reward is differentiable. To handle any scalar reward, we use a simple mechanism: generate a pair of images from the same starting noise but introduce stochasticity during sampling so that they diverge into two similar, counterfactual images.

<div class="caption" style="text-align: center; font-size: 13px; color: #666; margin-top: -4px; margin-bottom: 16px;">
  <b>Figure:</b> A pair of counterfactual images generated from the same initial noise with slight stochasticity. The two images share overall composition but differ in fine details, allowing their reward difference to estimate a gradient direction.

One image typically yields a higher reward than the other. The difference between the two images, weighted by their reward difference, gives us a direction that points from the worse image toward the better one. This is effectively a finite difference that tells us how reward varies along the direction the two images differ. We use this as our update direction and apply it uniformly across the flow, just as we did with the differentiable reward gradient. The paper analyzes this more rigorously, but the method itself is as simple as that.

## Highlighted Results

We tested FDFO by post-training Stable Diffusion 3.5 on three rewards: PickScore, VLM alignment, and their combination. PickScore is the easiest to optimize and both methods do well. VLM alignment is a noticeably harder reward, however, and FDFO pulls ahead significantly. On VLM alignment and the combined reward, FDFO reaches reward levels that Flow-GRPO is unable to attain.

Qualitative aspects of training also differ. Flow-GRPO periodically produces grid-like artifacts during extended training which fade in and out but can be severe. We don’t observe these with our method, even after equally long training. Flow-GRPO also shows consistent style drift across prompts, which we attribute to its noisier updates causing random mutation in reward-irrelevant dimensions. Our updates are cleaner, so the flow doesn't drift where it doesn't need to.

## Why This Beats the MDP Approach

- Current SOTA (Flow-GRPO, DanceGRPO) treats each denoising step as a separate MDP action
  - Each step's random perturbation (Gaussian noise injection) is an "action" drawn from a Gaussian policy
  - The advantage of the entire trajectory is attributed to every per-step perturbation
  - Updates pull velocities toward the random perturbations of high-reward trajectories

- **The likelihood problem**
  - Policy gradient methods weight updates by log-likelihood of the actions taken -- this is how they estimate the reward gradient
  - In LLMs this is natural: exact token-level log-probs come from the softmax distribution
  - In flow models, there is no clean analogue -- the "action" at each step is a continuous velocity, and the per-step likelihood under the flow is expensive/intractable to compute exactly
  - MDP methods handle this by imposing a Gaussian action distribution at each step and using that as a proxy likelihood, ignoring key properties of how likelihoods evolve under flows (e.g., divergence)
  - This is unprincipled -- it warps the problem to fit the machinery rather than fitting the machinery to the problem
  - FDFO sidesteps this entirely: we estimate the reward gradient directly via finite differences, never needing per-step likelihoods at all

- **The signal-to-noise problem**
  - If a trajectory gets high reward, *all* per-step perturbations get reinforced -- even though most were irrelevant to and orthogonal to reward ascent
  - Only a small fraction of the aggregate MDP update actually contributes to reward improvement; the rest is reward-neutral noise pushing the flow in random directions
  - This manifests as style drift during training -- the paper shows consistent style drift in Flow-GRPO images across all prompts tested
  - Eventually produces grid-like artifacts in Flow-GRPO that appear and disappear periodically during training; we never observe these artifacts even after equally long training
  - The noise also slowly accumulates into detrimental side effects, setting a cap on how much fine-tuning can be done in total

- **FDFO decouples the update from the random walk**
  - Stochasticity is used only to discover counterfactual sampling paths (exploring which image variations lead to higher reward)
  - The actual update is the clean, deterministic image difference weighted by reward difference -- no per-step noise in irrelevant dimensions
  - Every step receives an update that directly targets reward improvement
  - In Flow-GRPO, roughly half of the individual per-step flow updates may be detrimental to reward (pointing away from reward improvement)

## Results Highlights

- **Faster convergence**
  - FDFO converges significantly faster than Flow-GRPO across all three reward settings (PickScore, VLM alignment, combined)
  - Wall-clock speedup: 19x faster in baseline configuration (40 sampling steps) and 5x faster in fast configuration (10 steps) to cross a highlighted combined reward level
  - Gap widens with more complex rewards: VLM alignment is harder to optimize and Flow-GRPO starts to struggle while FDFO keeps improving

- **Higher ceiling**
  - Converges to higher rewards overall
  - On the combined reward (PickScore + VLM alignment / 10), FDFO reaches reward levels that Flow-GRPO never attains
  - Finite differences are perfectly competitive with true differentiable reward gradients (ablation rows E vs. I), showing no penalty from dropping differentiability

- **Fewer artifacts and less style drift**
  - No grid-like artifacts -- Flow-GRPO produces these periodically during extended training; FDFO never does, even after equally long training
  - Significantly less style drift -- Flow-GRPO images show consistent style drift across all prompts; we attribute this to its noisier flow updates causing random mutation
  - More stable training: the flow updates are cleaner, so reward-irrelevant dimensions do not drift freely

- **CFG becomes optional after post-training**
  - Before post-training, base model quality without CFG is very poor; CFG dramatically improves alignment and quality
  - After FDFO post-training without CFG, the model produces high-quality, well-aligned images on its own
  - Re-enabling CFG after post-training mainly compromises diversity and introduces the characteristic high-contrast look, with debatable benefit
  - Our optimization reaches similar reward levels quickly with or without CFG as a starting point
  - This saves the diversity cost and compute overhead of guidance at inference time

- **Control metrics confirm real improvements**
  - OneIG-Bench prompt alignment: combined reward (PickScore + VLM) significantly outperforms PickScore alone; FDFO outperforms Flow-GRPO
  - HPSv2 human preference: PickScore reward optimizes this well; combined reward comes out on top with FDFO
  - Diversity (OneIG-Bench): all rewards reduce diversity; diversity loss is approximately equally fast for both algorithms on an iso-reward basis; the combined reward with VLM causes the slowest diversity decline

- We are not giving a blanket recommendation to avoid CFG, but we present RL post-training as a promising alternative that can achieve similar benefits (trading diversity for alignment/quality) while offering full control over the optimization objective via reward design

## Closing

- FDFO is a simple first step toward understanding what makes RL post-training work well for diffusion models
  - It is a direct drop-in replacement for Flow-GRPO with faster convergence and higher-quality results
  - The full method is straightforward: generate paired trajectories from shared noise, compute reward-weighted image differences, apply them uniformly as velocity targets across all steps

- Our main takeaway: the structure of the generative process matters for how you do RL on it
  - Diffusion/flow models are not generic MDPs -- they have geometric structure (near-OT flows, coarse-to-fine denoising, approximately PSD Jacobians)
  - Working *with* this structure gives a simpler algorithm that performs better than forcing the problem into the MDP framework
  - By leaving the multi-step MDP formulation, we shorten the reward attribution horizon and expand the algorithmic design space

- Open questions and future directions
  - How to best retain diversity during reward optimization remains an open problem -- KL regularization is compatible with FDFO but not a complete solution
  - Aspects not explicitly rewarded are free to degenerate over time; making rewards comprehensive enough to eliminate the need for early stopping is an important challenge
  - User studies in downstream production settings have not yet been carried out

- Code and models will be released
