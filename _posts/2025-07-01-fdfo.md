We set out to find a simple, grounded RL post-training method for diffusion image generators. We made a few observations about the structure of diffusion flows that led to Finite Difference Flow Optimization, a new algorithm that converges faster, reaches higher rewards, and produces fewer artifacts than current solutions. This post walks through the enabling ideas and how we arrived at them.

<!-- ## Motivation: Why Base Diffusion Models Need Help -->

<!-- - Diffusion models learn to approximate the training data distribution via flow matching
  - They learn a velocity function $$v_\theta(\mathbf{x}; t, \mathbf{c})$$ that maps noise to images by solving an ODE backward in time from $$t=1$$ (pure noise) to $$t=0$$ (clean image)
  - The velocity points toward a weighted average of data points consistent with the current noisy image $$\mathbf{x}(t)$$

  Diffusion models fit the distribution of their training data, but they tend to do so conservatively. They assign non-zero likelihood to images that are near the data manifold but not on it, resulting in a distribution that envelops the natural image manifold without concentrating solely on real images. Smoothing the distribution isn't entirely bad, since it allows the model to generate unseen interpolations of training images rather than simply regurgitating them. But it's also what produces the washed-out, artifacted outputs you get from a base model.

This motivates the use of methods to sharpen the distribution. The dominant approach is classifier-free guidance (CFG), which concentrates mass toward higher-density modes at inference time. This gives you a knob to trade diversity for sample quality. But it's a coarse band-aid: you can adjust its strength but not the style or quality it imparts on the images, typically an over-saturated look. Interestingly, we find that RL post-training lets you make the same useful tradeoff while steering the model toward reward-specified behavior.

## RL as a Principled Alternative

In LLMs, RL post-training is the standard tool for this problem. Pretraining produces a model with broad coverage but a loose, unfocused output distribution. Techniques like RLHF and RLVR tighten that distribution onto useful outputs, which has driven major wins in alignment and reasoning. In image generation CFG analogously concentrates mass from a pretrained distribution into desirable regions. Because it has worked well enough, RL adoption in image generation has been slower than in language. But RL remains an appealing alternative since it can be steered with arbitrary scalar rewards from learned models or human preferences.

It isn't obivous how to apply RL to diffusion and flow models. Standard RL machinery was built in discrete domains where model likelihoods are easily accessible, but these are notoriously difficult to estimate in continuous flows. Existing approaches work around this by forcing the flow into an MDP framework with ungrounded proxy likelihoods. A natural question is whether we can ascend the reward gradient, the stated goal of policy gradient methods, in a more flow-native way.

  - Key difference: RL can be directed with arbitrary scalar rewards (human judgments, VLM scores, quality metrics); CFG is a loosely controlled intervention with no explicit objective -->

  - Existing approaches (Flow-GRPO, DanceGRPO) force the flow problem into an MDP framework with proxy Gaussian likelihoods at each step, to make standard RL machinery apply

FDFO is our simple first step toward analyzing and improving the RL post-training process for image generation.

## Setup: What We Are Working With

To introduce the enabling ideas behind our method, let's start with a motivating example. A flow matching model generates images by starting from random noise and taking a sequence of denoising steps to arrive at a clean image. Suppose we have a reward model, say, a vision-language model that we ask 'Does this image match the caption [CAPTION]? Answer Yes or No.' We can then use the log-probability of 'Yes' as a scalar reward. Our goal is to update the flow model so that it produces images that score higher under this reward (i.e., are high quality).

For this example, we backpropagate through the VLM to get a gradient in image space. This represents the direction to shift the output image to produce greater reward. Flow models generate images through multiple denoising steps, each with its own predicted velocity, so we need to translate our image-space gradient into update directions for every velocity prediction along the sampling chain. The obvious way to do this is to backpropagate through the chain itself. Methods like ReFL, DRaFT, and DRS have explored and refined variants of this, but there are a couple problems. The gradient has to traverse up the steps via the chain rule, and each step's Jacobian stretches, squeezes, and rotates it. By the time it reaches early steps, high frequencies are suppressed exponentially and some axes have exploded while others have vanished. Also, this assumes access to a differentiable reward model, which is a subset of the general RL case. Practically, this rules out direct human feedback or rewards extracted from real world.

- We start with a pre-trained flow matching model that generates images via multi-step denoising
  - Draw initial noise $$\boldsymbol{\epsilon} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I}_N)$$ of $$N$$ pixels
  - Solve the ODE $$\mathrm{d}\mathbf{x}(t) = v_\theta(\mathbf{x}; t, \mathbf{c})\,\mathrm{d}t$$ backward from $$t=1$$ to $$t=0$$ using $$T$$ discrete Euler steps
  - Each step produces a slightly less noisy intermediate image: $$\mathbf{x}_{i+1} = \mathbf{x}_i + (t_{i+1} - t_i)\,v_\theta(\mathbf{x}_i; t_i, \mathbf{c})$$
  - After $$T$$ steps, the initial noise is transformed into a generated image $$\mathbf{x}_T$$
  - Concretely: we use Stable Diffusion 3.5 Medium with LoRA layers, $$T=40$$ sampling steps, generating at 512x512 resolution

- Motivating example: we have access to a reward model
  - Concretely: feed the generated image + prompt to a VLM (Qwen2.5-VL-7B-Instruct), ask "Does this image match the caption '...'? Answer Yes or No."
  - Reward = $$100 \cdot \mathrm{sigmoid}(\mathrm{logits[\text{Yes}]} - \mathrm{logits[\text{No}]})$$
  - We also use PickScore (a CLIP-based model trained on human preferences) and their weighted combination: PickScore + VLM alignment / 10

- Goal: update the flow model's velocity function to produce higher-scoring images
  - Formally: $$\arg\max_\theta \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{c}\sim C,\,\mathbf{x}_0 \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})} R\big(f_\theta(\mathbf{x}_0; \mathbf{c})\big)$$
  - Geometrically: redirect the flow velocities so that a larger mass of noises maps to high-reward regions of image space
  - The reward may be non-differentiable or highly discontinuous

## The Naive Approach: Backprop Through the Chain

- Most obvious strategy: backpropagate the reward gradient through the entire sampling chain
  - Compute $$\partial R / \partial \mathbf{x}_T$$ -- a direction in image space that says "move this way to improve reward"
  - Backpropagate through the $$T$$-step sampling chain to get update directions for each step's velocity prediction
  - Methods like ReFL, DRaFT, and DRS have explored variants of this (supervising at a random step, last few steps, or sparse subset of steps)

- This works, but three serious problems arise:
  - **Stability**: the gradient traverses $$T$$ steps via chain rule; each step's Jacobian stretches, squeezes, and rotates it; by early steps the gradient is poorly conditioned -- some directions explode, others vanish
  - **Speed**: must store all intermediate activations across $$T$$ steps for backpropagation -- expensive in both memory and compute ($$T$$ backward passes through the denoiser)
  - **Differentiability**: the entire pipeline including the reward model must be differentiable; this rules out human feedback, external tool APIs, VLMs behind cloud endpoints -- any non-differentiable interaction

- Our ablation (row J in the paper) confirms: backpropagating the true reward gradient through the sampling trajectory is *significantly worse* than our approach of applying a uniform update direction
  - The accumulated Jacobian distortions actively hurt performance

## Key Insight: Flows Are Approximately Non-Rotational

- What is backprop through the chain actually doing at each step?
  - At step $$i$$, the Jacobian $$\mathbf{J}_i(\mathbf{x}_i)$$ of the remaining flow maps the image-space gradient into the correct update direction for that step
  - It answers: "given that we want to shift the final image in direction $$\mathbf{g}$$, how should we shift the intermediate image at step $$i$$?"

- When is an approximate direction still useful for gradient descent?
  - An update direction $$\mathbf{d}$$ is a valid descent direction if it has positive inner product with the true gradient: $$\mathbf{d}^\top \mathbf{g} > 0$$
  - Geometrically: $$\mathbf{d}$$ must lie in the same half-space -- angle with the true gradient must be less than 90 degrees
  - This half-space condition holds whenever the Jacobian is positive semi-definite (PSD): it can stretch and scale the vector but does not rotate it past 90 degrees

- Why we expect this to hold for diffusion flows
  - Flow matching is designed for low-curvature, nearly straight flows -- high curvature requires more integration steps and increases cost
  - Optimal transport (OT) flows are straight-line paths with PSD Jacobians by construction
  - Learned diffusion flows are not exactly OT, but multiple studies find strong numerical similarity between them (Khrulkov et al. "Understanding DDPMs as latent codes")
  - The paper's formal condition: $$\Delta\mathbf{x}^\top \mathbf{J}_i(\mathbf{x}_i) \Delta\mathbf{x} \geq 0$$ -- the Jacobian of the mapping induced by the remaining flow does not flip the difference vector to the opposing half-space
  - This would hold exactly if the flow were an OT coupling

## The Simple Alternative: Copy the Gradient to Every Step

- Instead of backpropagating through the chain, use the image-space gradient directly as the update direction at *every* step
  - Same direction everywhere, no per-step Jacobian computation needed
  - Valid descent direction at all steps (assuming approximate PSD, per the argument above)

- Why this works *better* than the exact backpropagated approach -- a bias-variance tradeoff
  - The copied gradient is biased (not the precisely correct update for each intermediate step)
  - But it has dramatically better conditioning -- no accumulated distortions from composing $$T$$ Jacobians
  - The exact backpropagated gradient suffers from vanishing/exploding components after multiplying many matrices

- The denoiser's low-pass filtering effect makes the exact gradient worse
  - Since the diffusion model is a denoiser, the Jacobian of each sampling step acts as a low-pass filter -- it attenuates high-frequency components more than low-frequency ones
  - Composing $$T$$ of these filters squashes high-frequency components exponentially
  - The gradient arriving at early steps is dominated by low-frequency structure and has lost fine-grained directional information
  - The copied gradient preserves the full frequency spectrum at every step -- clean, consistent signal throughout, just like in pre-training

- Paper ablations confirm this directly
  - Row I: true reward gradient (via differentiable reward), applied uniformly across steps -- comparable to our full method
  - Row J: true reward gradient + backprop through the sampling trajectory -- significantly worse
  - The backprop Jacobians hurt more than they help

## Dropping Differentiability: Finite Differences

- Everything so far still needs a differentiable reward model to compute the image-space gradient $$\nabla_\mathbf{x} R$$
- We want to work with *any* scalar reward -- the standard RL scenario (human feedback, VLM APIs, non-differentiable metrics)

- Solution: approximate the gradient with finite differences between paired trajectories
  - Generate two images from the same starting noise $$\mathbf{x}_0 = \hat{\mathbf{x}}_0 = \boldsymbol{\epsilon}$$
  - Introduce small stochasticity during sampling so the trajectories diverge in fine details (e.g., different facial expression, different flower arrangement) while keeping overall composition similar
  - We use a uniform stochasticity schedule of $$\gamma_i = 0.0025$$ at each step, adapted from the EDM stochastic sampler -- this overshoots to a lower noise level then adds fresh noise to land at the correct level
  - Score both output images with the reward model: $$R(\mathbf{x}_T)$$ and $$R(\hat{\mathbf{x}}_T)$$
  - Compute the difference vector $$\Delta\mathbf{x} = \hat{\mathbf{x}}_T - \mathbf{x}_T$$ and the reward difference $$\Delta R = R(\hat{\mathbf{x}}_T) - R(\mathbf{x}_T)$$
  - The product $$\Delta R \cdot \Delta\mathbf{x}$$ always points from the lower-reward image toward the higher-reward one (the sign of $$\Delta R$$ automatically flips the direction)
  - Normalize to cancel the squared magnitude effect: $$\overline{\Delta\mathbf{x}} = \Delta\mathbf{x} / (\|\Delta\mathbf{x}\|_\mathrm{RMS}^2 + 10^{-6})$$

- Apply this normalized, reward-weighted difference as the update direction for all steps along both trajectories, exactly as in the previous section

- This is the full method: **Finite Difference Flow Optimization (FDFO)**
  - Finite difference between paired samples estimates the reward gradient direction
  - Applied uniformly across all steps of the flow
  - Row I vs. Row E in ablations: finite differences are perfectly competitive with true per-image differentiation of the reward, confirming this is a good approximation regardless of reward differentiability

## Why This Beats the MDP Approach

- Current SOTA (Flow-GRPO, DanceGRPO) treats each denoising step as a separate MDP action
  - Each step's random perturbation (Gaussian noise injection) is an "action" drawn from a Gaussian policy
  - The advantage of the entire trajectory is attributed to every per-step perturbation
  - Updates pull velocities toward the random perturbations of high-reward trajectories

- **The likelihood problem**
  - Policy gradient methods weight updates by log-likelihood of the actions taken -- this is how they estimate the reward gradient
  - In LLMs this is natural: exact token-level log-probs come from the softmax distribution
  - In flow models, there is no clean analogue -- the "action" at each step is a continuous velocity, and the per-step likelihood under the flow is expensive/intractable to compute exactly
  - MDP methods handle this by imposing a Gaussian action distribution at each step and using that as a proxy likelihood, ignoring key properties of how likelihoods evolve under flows (e.g., divergence)
  - This is unprincipled -- it warps the problem to fit the machinery rather than fitting the machinery to the problem
  - FDFO sidesteps this entirely: we estimate the reward gradient directly via finite differences, never needing per-step likelihoods at all

- **The signal-to-noise problem**
  - If a trajectory gets high reward, *all* per-step perturbations get reinforced -- even though most were irrelevant to and orthogonal to reward ascent
  - Only a small fraction of the aggregate MDP update actually contributes to reward improvement; the rest is reward-neutral noise pushing the flow in random directions
  - This manifests as style drift during training -- the paper shows consistent style drift in Flow-GRPO images across all prompts tested
  - Eventually produces grid-like artifacts in Flow-GRPO that appear and disappear periodically during training; we never observe these artifacts even after equally long training
  - The noise also slowly accumulates into detrimental side effects, setting a cap on how much fine-tuning can be done in total

- **FDFO decouples the update from the random walk**
  - Stochasticity is used only to discover counterfactual sampling paths (exploring which image variations lead to higher reward)
  - The actual update is the clean, deterministic image difference weighted by reward difference -- no per-step noise in irrelevant dimensions
  - Every step receives an update that directly targets reward improvement
  - In Flow-GRPO, roughly half of the individual per-step flow updates may be detrimental to reward (pointing away from reward improvement)

## Results Highlights

- **Faster convergence**
  - FDFO converges significantly faster than Flow-GRPO across all three reward settings (PickScore, VLM alignment, combined)
  - Wall-clock speedup: 19x faster in baseline configuration (40 sampling steps) and 5x faster in fast configuration (10 steps) to cross a highlighted combined reward level
  - Gap widens with more complex rewards: VLM alignment is harder to optimize and Flow-GRPO starts to struggle while FDFO keeps improving

- **Higher ceiling**
  - Converges to higher rewards overall
  - On the combined reward (PickScore + VLM alignment / 10), FDFO reaches reward levels that Flow-GRPO never attains
  - Finite differences are perfectly competitive with true differentiable reward gradients (ablation rows E vs. I), showing no penalty from dropping differentiability

- **Fewer artifacts and less style drift**
  - No grid-like artifacts -- Flow-GRPO produces these periodically during extended training; FDFO never does, even after equally long training
  - Significantly less style drift -- Flow-GRPO images show consistent style drift across all prompts; we attribute this to its noisier flow updates causing random mutation
  - More stable training: the flow updates are cleaner, so reward-irrelevant dimensions do not drift freely

- **CFG becomes optional after post-training**
  - Before post-training, base model quality without CFG is very poor; CFG dramatically improves alignment and quality
  - After FDFO post-training without CFG, the model produces high-quality, well-aligned images on its own
  - Re-enabling CFG after post-training mainly compromises diversity and introduces the characteristic high-contrast look, with debatable benefit
  - Our optimization reaches similar reward levels quickly with or without CFG as a starting point
  - This saves the diversity cost and compute overhead of guidance at inference time

- **Control metrics confirm real improvements**
  - OneIG-Bench prompt alignment: combined reward (PickScore + VLM) significantly outperforms PickScore alone; FDFO outperforms Flow-GRPO
  - HPSv2 human preference: PickScore reward optimizes this well; combined reward comes out on top with FDFO
  - Diversity (OneIG-Bench): all rewards reduce diversity; diversity loss is approximately equally fast for both algorithms on an iso-reward basis; the combined reward with VLM causes the slowest diversity decline

- We are not giving a blanket recommendation to avoid CFG, but we present RL post-training as a promising alternative that can achieve similar benefits (trading diversity for alignment/quality) while offering full control over the optimization objective via reward design

## Closing

- FDFO is a simple first step toward understanding what makes RL post-training work well for diffusion models
  - It is a direct drop-in replacement for Flow-GRPO with faster convergence and higher-quality results
  - The full method is straightforward: generate paired trajectories from shared noise, compute reward-weighted image differences, apply them uniformly as velocity targets across all steps, with SPO clipping for on-policy stability

- Our main takeaway: the structure of the generative process matters for how you do RL on it
  - Diffusion/flow models are not generic MDPs -- they have geometric structure (near-OT flows, coarse-to-fine denoising, approximately PSD Jacobians)
  - Working *with* this structure gives a simpler algorithm that performs better than forcing the problem into the MDP framework
  - By leaving the multi-step MDP formulation, we shorten the reward attribution horizon and expand the algorithmic design space

- Open questions and future directions
  - How to best retain diversity during reward optimization remains an open problem -- KL regularization is compatible with FDFO but not a complete solution
  - Crafting VLM-based reward components that target diversity directly could be promising
  - Aspects not explicitly rewarded are free to degenerate over time; making rewards comprehensive enough to eliminate the need for early stopping is an important challenge
  - User studies in downstream production settings have not yet been carried out

- Code and models will be released
